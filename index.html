WHY NFTS?

As a new, decentralized art form, NFTs are making art-making more accessible to anyone with enough money to mint one. Because of how much energy is necessary to so, and because of the hype around blockchain tech, some NFTs have sold for millions of dollars. With NFTs, small artists have a chance to finally get rich quick - so naturally, most people who mint NFTs are not artists, and NFTs have a reputation for being aesthetically unpleasant. They're usually made of copy-pasted assets, are procedurally generated, or use stolen art, since you can mint any digital work on the Internet, even if it's not your own.

WHAT DOES IT COST?

An analysis of 18,000 NFTs showed that the “average NFT has a carbon footprint… equivalent to more than a month’s worth of electricity for a person living in the EU.” This is due to the nature of cryptocurrency mining, specifically for Ethereum, which is the currency exchanged in most major NFT marketplaces: “[t]o keep financial records secure, the system forces people to solve complex puzzles using energy-guzzling machines.” For every mined crypto token, the cost of mining the next increases; if you have been unable to purchase a graphics card recently, chances are it’s due to blockchain enthusiasts buying out GPUs to feed their money-making machine factories. Also, artists have made their art accounts private or deleted them altogether to avoid having their work stolen.

WHAT IS IT WORTH?

Some NFTs have sold for millions, but their popularity is decreasing the more people realize that they’re not actually worth anything - an NFT is not an original copy of a digital work, but a link to a digital receipt that can be deleted or changed at any time. They’ve been used as a way to launder money and scam those looking to invest in new tech, though. Since the majority of NFTs are made in bulk, they're not only ugly but devoid of life or meaning, like a weird form of hypercapitalist anti-art.

WHO DOES IT BENEFIT?

The people into NFTs tend to be white men with enough money or means to invest in them. A big reason the internet was so novel and freeing when it was first created is that users were anonymous; it was a place where you weren't judged by your real-life status, wealth, or identity. When people shared their art, they did it to bond with others. But much of the NFT craze is about using your collections of hyper-expensive clipart to show off your wealth (twitter, for example, changes the shape of your profile picture if it's an NFT), essentially bringing in the passing-around of capital between investors that plagues the fine art world today into the digital scene. It's a profitable way to reproduce existing socioeconomic divisions in a space where they were not meant to exist.
